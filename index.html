Accessible Components

Accessible components are those that are designed with the needs of as many users as possible in mind. This includes keyboard navigation, screen reader support, and other accessibility features.

Edited State Diagrams

My revised state diagram for keyboard interaction. I have tweaked the way escape works slightly so that most of the time it will simply close the dropdown outright. This improves learnability, since it aligns more closely with typical escape functionality, but it could also make the interface less efficient for keyboard users who often switch between options in the menu, since it eliminates a method of traversing to the top.

My revised state diagram for mouse interaction makes use of right click for different functionality. While in the original implementation, right and left click both took the user to the page corresponding to the suggestion, right click now removes the suggestion instead. This makes a tradeoff between efficiency and learnability, since it might be confusing for some users who right click only to find their option gone.

Takeaways/Questions

Google and Amazon did well with the simplicity of their designs and bold color palettes, which made their menus easily learnable and fairly accessible visually. I've tried to maintain the overall look and palette of the menu, while making some slight visual tweaks for more clarity. I took inspiration from Discord's effective use of instructive symbols in my choice of button icon for the preview. I chose to make it always visible as a clear indicator of the functionality, as opposed to the existing designs, which only show the buttons on hovering a suggestion.

My revised component solves for a couple mismatches. One is with users who use a screenreader, or even just the keyboard, to navigate, for whom it is especially inefficient to be constantly switching pages entirely. It would also help people who aren't adept at formulating google searches to quickly find the terms that best align with their intentions, rather than sending them down a rabbit hole.

On the mobile Discord application, the button to open the search filters menu was offset from the search bar did not contrast very well with the surroundings. On the one hand, people with visual impairments might have a hard time locating the button, especially if they don't already know about it. On the other, the automatic appearance of the dropdown below the search bars on Amazon and Google could lead to visual clutter and confusion (especially because they use the same palettes as the search bar) for someone with poor eyesight, or even someone who's simply in a hurry.

Google's focus order had some extra steps, for example needing to press escape twice to exit the dropdown (once moved back to the top option). While this could allow for more flexibility for frequent keyboard users, I could see the departure from some norms, ie. of escape exiting a component, being confusing, especially for someone using a screen reader.

It seems like interfaces are mostly designed for mouse/trackpad users and mobile users. The mobile interfaces were quite different to the desktop ones, which suggests to me that they may be worked on by separate teams. When it came to keyboard vs. mouse, however, the visual language of the components lent themselves better to mouse interaction than keyboard. On discord, for example, I could see a keyboard/screen-reader user being very confused by the options presented to them through the focus order.
